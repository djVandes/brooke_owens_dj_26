Debugging My Internship Experience:
Intentionality and Innovation

Brooke Owens Class of 2026 Fellowship Application

THE STORY

It's no secret that interns don't always get the most exciting work.

At the begining of my internship at Marvell, found myself in exactly this position. I knew this was a large company with interesting projects happening beyond my desk. Though I knew the tasks I had were important, their monotonous nature left me unmotivated and disenchanted with engineering. I feared this was all the next sixteen months had in store.

So I made a choice; actively seek out the unknown.

I met with my manager to discuss additional research opportunities and proposed that I explore AI applications in hardware design. Though initially wary about my juggling this alongside my primary responsibilities, my manager agreed and supported me in these new projects. I then had exciting oportunities to connect with researchers all over North America working on AI for hardware. Together we found a gap in the current literature, a reliable code generation tool for the notoriously difficult syntax of Verilog.

My new target was clear!

I had established a new goal, to create an AI code-generating tool using AWS to solve Marvell's ongoing problem of unreliable chatbots to assist hardware design. Though balancing these new oppotunities while performing my regular tasks required careful time management, through detailed documentation of my progress, I could quickly switch between my design work and AI research. With the feedback and support from senior engineers at Marvell, I engineered prompts, compared language models, and iterated on workflows relentlessly.

The hard part begins, trial and error, and error, and error

Iteration after iteration failed basic syntax tests. I tried training a private model on the company's codebase, I tested the newest models ranked highest on code-generating leaderboards, but nothing worked! No matter how carefully I designed the single-agent system, it couldn't produce syntactically correct, compilable code without serious intervention. At that point, manual coding was faster. I considered giving up.

And finally, a breakthrough!

Then I discovered multi-agent AI in the form of interacting language models that could self-correct with human oversight but little intervention. After experimenting with different agent workflows and prompts, I completed DIVA (Debugging AI Verilog Agents), my first ever research project!
I presented it at an internal conference in Boston. Here, I finally met face-to-face the engineers throughout North America I had been working with remotely for over six months and many others who were interested by my work. While Marvell didn't adopt DIVA company-wide, the project successfully generated functional RSA encryption code from documentation, something previous systems the company was considering couldn't do, and brought multi-agent workflows to the table.
